Subject: Inevitable?

by Michael Frydman

I'm starting to feel that CDZI - particularly with its newly refreshed balance sheet (more to
come?) - can emerge from this virus market, as an indispensable name for many personal
and even small institutional portfolios.  No matter what the post-crisis economy looks like, I'm certain one lasting impact in the markets will be an elevated focus on severe macro risk, more specifically climate change and as a result, it's increasingly plausible to imagine a market that applies an even larger valuation premium to the shares.

My developing view starts with stories like in the link below - mega-drought in the Western US linked to climate change, 2.0 - and ends with the market taking a completely differently
approach to them than it did just few years ago when severe drought was a weekly story for
months, even years.  I think it highly unlikely that the market will ever go back to the
measured way it views these potentially high-impact issues, especially within the climate
change theme. C19 has shown that this shit is real and I think there is now a reasonable
chance that Cadiz is already being looked at as more than just the answer to a short-term
problem, more than a survivor, which just trades off the next political showdown or pipeline
financing, as important as those milestones are.

Instead, I think in the post-virus market, Cadiz will increasingly be viewed as 'inevitable', a
share investors need to have in their portfolios because it owns a piece of the future, the way
they might now own Tesla.  When you've stopped laughing, name me another old economy
name that offers investors exposure to an UPSIDE to climate change (sorry, but its true), in
an essential business immune to shutdowns, which for good measure, remains a 'named'
project in any Trump infrastructure bill.  Several of you have commented on how well the
stock has performed in this chaos, and I think that, at least in part, the share is starting to
attract that kind of shareholder; ie., one that is looking 5/10 years down the line and sees
massive potential, unencumbered by any of the market's current headwinds, without
necessarily waiting around to figure out what that huge value might be exactly.  That kind of
buyer tends to have far less interest in shorter-term valuation concerns to say the least.

Cadiz's EV is already the better part of $1/2B; i.e., whether we like it or not, quite a bit of 'good news' is already anticipated.  A wholesale debt restructuring that provides significant working capital would be a welcome catalyst (though it's by no means necessary at present.  I'm confident that the company can fund the option on and development of the Northern pipeline - the only significant immediate capital need - independently if necessary), early planting of hemp demonstrating that that business can provide significant yearly EBITDA, and/or further breakthroughs with the BLM that revives (raises from the dead?) the prospects of Stage I (!), will all constitute major events and will certainly cause the share price to surge, at least in the immediate aftermath.  What will sustain those gains however, I believe, is how each of those milestones add to the market's view of permanence for Cadiz and its now growing number of business lines.

The secret sauce however, is what happens next, and then after that, if you will. When the internet bubble burst in the late 90s, some ridiculous percentage of web-based companies valued at double-digit multiples of sales and billions in market cap went to 0.  Of course after this monumental loss of wealth, the market became far more circumspect and sceptical on new offerings in the space and in valuing the survivors.  Price-to-sales became a four-letter word among analysts and investors, if not eliminated altogether.  The impact was so extensive that it seemed every MBA I interviewed in those days was promoting themselves as the second coming of Jim Chanos.  Almost no one coming into the Equity business wanted to own shares, just find new names and areas to go short.

For me, it was that increasingly unforgiving backdrop that made Amazon's (and scores of other anointed names I'm sure) recovery from the late 90s crash not just impressive, but illustrative of what could happen to a name like Cadiz in today's market.  For a decade, there was nearly a constant hue and cry about how this bookseller - which was losing money on every book it sold but was trying to make it up with volume - could continue enjoying a share price recovery while requiring pre-crash rationale to justify it.  It took a while but the penny finally dropped; the early adopters in Amazon didn't own the shares because the book business or its progeny were suddenly going to become hugely profitable as bricks and mortar competitors disappeared,  Rather, the sceptics began to realise that those stubborn early investors saw a future where Amazon 'owned' a corner of the universe that had not even been invented yet, the value of that being almost beyond measure, and they began to cover their shorts and much more importantly, to actually start buying the shares as an essential part of their portfolios.  The same pattern has of course been repeated more recently with Tesla, where eventually the market has largely clocked that Elon Musk could give 2 F's about making cars; in the end, he has designs on fundamentally redefining how electricity itself is created, stored and distributed, among his LESS ambitious goals.  Sure the 'Amazon'-style battle was ostensibly won when Tesla showed it could make money in its car business last year, but was that worth the share price tripling? I don't think so.

Cadiz an old economy version of Tesla?  Crazy right?  Scott Slater has no plans for world domination that only an elite minority can see at present, fair enough.  But coming off the exigent circumstances of this virus, is it really such an intellectual leap to imagine a world where the 11 trillion gallons of water beneath the company's property begin to be seen by the market as a near mission critical resource and, rather than a one or 2 hit wonder (e.g., hemp + Northern sales), the company's entire asset base comes in for genuine consideration: storage's vast potential is seen as when not if but when, Stage I is suddenly seen as more likely than not to go ahead, rather than a cold case as it is now, and even the original EIR limits might have to be reconsidered, not because the company seeks it but because the developing crisis may demand it.  What kind of valuation premium might accrue to the shares in that environment?  I've no idea, but I note that the battle over the 'right' way to look at Amazon took around 10 years. The struggle over Tesla’s valuation took little more than two.
