Life is Math

by Michael Frydman

Life is math.
I appreciate that more every day. It’s so meta that even the statement itself is
math, ‘is’ being the English language equivalent of an equal sign. Life ‘equals’
math and then, as everyone remembers, math must equal life.

Unfortunately, according to the scientific method, Life is Math would be my null
hypothesis, and I would be required to then prove the statement subject to
constraints, employ a lot of differential equations and then worst of all, show my
work. That’s not gonna happen. Tragically, my Imaginary Engineering degree
got me only far enough to realise, at 58, that there’s something wonderous here
but proving it is sadly beyond my reach.

For now Life is Math is simply going to have to remain one of those amazing
insights I simply know is true, rather than something I can prove. Or more
commonly, something I can demonstrate, not validate. Casting off the burden
of proof is of course, liberating. At that point, one is left with the most
wonderous collection of anecdotes, examples and illustrations – ironically
anathema for the mathematician – many of which can energise both the God-
fearing and atheist in equal measure.

Finding Fibonacci sequences in everything from seashells to trading patterns
in the stock market, or being able to apply Chevychevs inequality to
population growth is heady stuff, but in the end, leaves me a bit flat. No, the
life/math intersection that truly resonates for me derives from the
breakthroughs in Probability Theory, the world of John Nash and A Beautiful
Mind, to the almost poetic precision of Stephen Jay Gould in Freakanomics,
essentially a collections of common sense essays devoted to demonstrating
one of Maths single greatest contributions to Life to date: the importance of
distinguishing between correlation and causation as human nature
(particularly among Republicans) so often wrongly (and disasterously)
defaults to the latter by misunderstanding the former.

One need look no further than the very virus that now so dominates our Lives
to see what I mean. In many ways, Covid19 is the coming out for Probability
Theory and the dangers of confusing what appears to be correlation (say
between human activities and the disease) and true causation. At the very
beginning of the world coming to grips with the pandemic, we were being
lectured about how humans are naturally wired to act on the fear of the
possible over the far more likely embrace of the probable. Even though, like
everyone else, most of me was immediately gripped by fear for my loved
ones, part of me rejoiced because my favourite Math/Life overlap had hit the
big time.

Now, weeks later, we have angry, Trump-backed activists trying to Make
America Dead Again by rejecting the very models based on the Math I love,
and instead embracing meaningless anecdotal evidence and conspiracies,
endangering everyone. Math is Life. Perhaps Ive proved it afterall.
