Corrupt intent

by Michael Frydman

Echoridge
20 May '20 - 10:48 - 49463 of 49466   Edit 0  1 0
Libel is a toughie for sure, but can a Company pursue individuals for corrupt intent?

'Should' it, is of course also something to consider but, you know, I'm just wondering: Corrupt intent would so elegantly capture pattern(s) of behaviour over an extended period of time - someone's life's work, if you will - of not just outright lies and/or breath- taking errors without correction, but frequent, repeated instances of wilful misrepresentations and distortions of good news and positive progress (Americans call it 'Swiftboating', basically a purposeful effort to attack an opponent's strengths - like a positive press release for a company - through a deceptive process of ignoring the obvious merits and creating an alternative narrative, using baseless innuendo and phoney 'new' information in order to do harm to its or its executives' reputations. An example here might be, oh I don't know...just spitballin'... but maybe hinting darkly that there was more than an infinitesimal chance of a special dividend being cancelled after the shares have gone ex, or - and this is gonna sound so crazy I know, but try and stay with me - like where someone might, like many many times, impute sinister intentions by company management in writing that the money that Company has been receiving on time and in full in repayment of a debt - cash that is effectively allowing it to have purchased a valuable asset for free, and then to set up massive returns of capital to its shareholders - is instead, in part, 'going to money heaven', which is a suuuuper subtle reference but - maybe its just me - could just be misinterpreted, you know?), which is sufficiently extensive as to constitute a clear effort to harm the company's share price and therefore denude a crucial foundation of its capital structure; ie., bring harm to that company. Then, if that behavioural pattern can be demonstrated - and in some cases, that would not be too difficult - then surely demonstrating the use of multiple aliases (which would itself constitute of violation of a platform's code of conduct) by this malefactor(s) would extend and deepen the evidence of his/their malicious intentions. And here's the final neat, little observation of this theoretical undertaking: financial motivation need not be a direct motive. In other words, the perpetrator(s) don't have to be short the stock, nor do they even have to own now worthless shares, making revenge their motive for engaging in such a malevolent effort over a long period of time. In fact, corrupt intent would easily capture 'just wanting to see the world burn', colloquially known as 'Committing A Joker' as a recognised motive for such malign behaviour.

Sorry, don't mind me. I was just, you know, wondering.
