Ringo

by Kate Frydman

Without a reason to leave my place, it was probable I would stay there forever.

I pulled myself out of bed and walked room to room and back again, penning my checklist which was meant to be like a vertiginous escape plan. When it was clear that my to-dos were largely reminders to eat, (something I did not need to brave the world for, thank you Postmates) I closed my eyes and told myself I needed a new outfit immediately, repeating it again and again until it became a comforting refrain. I conjured a phony occasion with an imminent date and convinced myself that without anything to wear, it would be another place and another group of people I would never see; I needed to leave now so I could leave then.

Out the door and up the first hill I went, trying to ignore how immediately my calves ached, how my breath became shallow. Two more hills to go. Hands in pockets, eyes trained ahead, looking through everything, feeling like water. I knew the way of this mission: I would visit the over priced, earth toned shops on Sunset Boulevard and spend $60 on a linen crop top. Whatever the shirt didn’t solve would then be reliant on the sales person’s approval - not their approval of the purchase, mind you, but of me as a human being. I would expect that the stranger in the shop to like me for me.

After the purchase I would leave the shop, stand at the street corner and angle my face towards the sun, willing it to hit my cheek just right, nature’s way of telling me it was all going to be okay. Or maybe it was just nature’s way of telling me it was still there, and if I was there to confirm that it was still there, then we’d both be there together. Is that comforting? Sort of a vague intimacy. Sun kissed.

On the way up the second hill I passed by a man. Propped up on his modest patch of grass, the man’s cheeks were flushed red, his belly swelling and shrinking, unbothered that his skin was slowly cooking. Behind him, the man's Silver Lake home kept watch from its second story advantage. It seemed to say to me ‘keep it moving.’ I wanted to tell the house that I lived just a few doors down but I sensed it wouldn’t matter.

In the man’s arm laid his dog, a mid sized husky mix, also on its side, also enjoying the increasingly oppressive heat. The dog was elegant, understated, and their les affair faire attitude indicated a celebrity name like Rihanna, or Prince, or maybe Ringo if the owner was self-effacing. The dog closed their eyes to the sun, soaking it in. Like this dog had had a difficult day at work and was finally taken a moment for themselves. Like they were slowing down for once.

I kept it moving. I knew that in all likelihood I would never see that man or his work-life balanced dog again. It was a small neighborhood, but I had never seen the same neighbor twice. Nothing has ever seemed to stick in my surroundings. Always blurring, always changing, I move through them and when I turn away, they’re gone.
