Passover

by Kate Frydman

INT. DINING ROOM. EVENING.

The Bergen-Silver-Goldensteins sit around their white table cloth Sedar, a colourful spread that lays untouched. The family is tense and shifty, avoiding eye-contact, tapping on their phones.

At the head of the table we have MARSHA (50s, matriarch, already exhausted), who closely watches her husband DAVID (50s, lovable, easily antagonized) who in turn has his eyes trained on GRANDMA (David’s mother, rough around the edges and one giant spike in the middle).

The kids, BENNIE (13, theatrical), NATALIE (15, affable and detached) and SAM (17, sardonic) wait quietly for an adult to say something.

Marsha finally breaks the silence.

MARSHA
Okay Bennie, why don’t you go ahead and start?

Bennie takes this moment with gusto.

BENNIE
Alright ya’ll, welcome back. Let me start off by asking the obvious: how is this night different from all other nights? Well! On all other nights--

DAVID
Am I really such a bad son?

MARSHA
Oh Jesus.

NATALIE
(finger guns)
Not sure we saved him a seat.

SAM
Mmm was that clever though?

NATALIE
Pretty sure it was, yeah.

DAVID
Come on ma, let’s just talk about this so we can put it behind us and start fresh. That’s what Passover is all about...
